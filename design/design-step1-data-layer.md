## Problem Statement

Build a World Cup match predictor that outputs win/draw/loss probabilities, goal
counts, and over/under for each match. The user intends to actually bet his own
money on the output, and predictions may influence others who also bet. The user
explicitly scoped THIS session to **Step 1: gather the best possible, cleanest,
richest football data** — the modeling and edge-testing come later. He dislikes
treating a 2010 match the same as a 2026 match (different teams, different
players) and wants recent data weighted more heavily, plus deep per-player and
per-team stats (striker goals this/last season, keeper goals conceded, pass
completion %, possession, total shots, shots on target, lineups, injuries).

## Demand Evidence

This is a personal tool, so "demand" is the user's own conviction plus real
financial stakes. The user was clear and repeated three times that the data
layer is what he wants to build first ("First i just want to be able to gather
the best possible data... this is step 1"). The honest read: demand for the DATA
is real and self-evident; demand for a *profitable* predictor is unproven and
gated on the edge test deferred to a later session.

## Status Quo (the real competitor)

The thing being bet against is the **betting market** (Pinnacle, Bet365, soft
books), not "stale historical data." Closing odds already price in injuries,
lineups, form, xG, possession models, and player stats, aggregated by
professionals with paid feeds. At the World Cup, market attention is at its
maximum — the hardest possible place to out-predict sharp books. The market's
implied probability (after removing the bookmaker margin / vig of ~4-6%) is the
benchmark any model must beat to make money. Raw accuracy is not the metric;
beating the closing line is.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

- **User:** the builder himself, betting his own money; secondarily friends who
  may follow his picks.
- **Narrowest wedge (for the eventual product):** collect bookmaker odds across
  many books, compute an independent probability, and only act where the gap is
  large — most plausibly on soft/recreational books that move slowly. Not
  "predict every match well."
- **Wedge for THIS session (data layer):** a clean, queryable football dataset
  with a `match_id` spine, dated facts (so recency-weighting is trivial later),
  team + player advanced stats, AND bookmaker odds stored alongside every match.

## Constraints

- Solo developer, local-first, experimental budget.
- Time pressure: the 2026 World Cup is underway; the live test window is now.
- Player-level data richness is desired but most player stats are only partially
  predictive and largely already priced by the market.
- Entity resolution (matching team/player names across sources) is the real
  difficulty, not downloading data.

## Premises

1. The betting MARKET, not stale data, is the thing to beat — so bookmaker odds
   are stored alongside every match from day one. (User did not affirm; recorded
   as the designer's strong recommendation and embedded as a schema column, not a
   blocking debate.)
2. The user's recency intuition = time-decay weighting (Dixon-Coles `exp(-ξ·t)`)
   + Elo ratings. These are the proven backbone; player stats are secondary
   signal. (Validated for the user.)
3. Edge, if any, most likely comes from soft/recreational books being slow — not
   from out-predicting sharp books at the World Cup. (Designer recommendation,
   deferred to later session.)
4. Success is eventually measured by "would this have beaten the closing line /
   made money," never raw accuracy. (Designer recommendation; user kept focus on
   data for now.)
5. The richest recent-form signal for a national-team player lives in his CLUB
   matches (FBref/StatsBomb), joined to the national lineup via the player.
   National matches are too rare to carry form. (Designer insight, accepted.)
   CAVEAT: on FREE data this signal is sparse — StatsBomb open data covers few
   club competitions and FBref scraping is fragile. Full club-form coverage for
   most national-team players likely requires the paid API-Football path. Decide
   at the budget question whether the recency value prop justifies paying.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: One API to rule them all (API-Football / API-Sports)
- Summary: Single paid API for results, lineups, injuries, player stats, and odds.
- Effort: S. Risk: Low-Med.
- Pros: One integration, one schema, fastest to a working dataset, odds included.
- Cons: ~$15-30/mo, rate limits, less control over historical depth, vendor lock.
- Reuses: nothing yet (greenfield).

### Approach B: Best-of-breed composite
- Summary: football-data.co.uk (historical odds) + FBref/StatsBomb (xG,
  possession, passes, keeper) + martj42/international_results (spine) + computed
  national-team Elo.
- Effort: L/XL. Risk: Med/High.
- Pros: Richest possible, mostly free, full control.
- Cons: 4+ scrapers, brutal entity-resolution burden, slowest to first value.
- Reuses: the user's recommended international_results repo as the spine.

### Approach C: Pragmatic hybrid (RECOMMENDED)
- Summary: Phase the build. Spine first (international_results + computed
  national-team Elo), then odds (The Odds API), then richness (FBref/StatsBomb
  player + team stats).
- Effort: M. Risk: Med (entity resolution — the "real difficulty" per Constraints
  — is deferred by phasing, not eliminated; it stays Med on that axis).
- Pros: Working dataset in days; richness grows in phases; backtestable as soon
  as odds land; uses the user's repo as the spine.
- Cons: Multiple sources still need entity resolution (mitigated by phasing).
- Reuses: international_results spine + computed national-team Elo.

## Recommended Approach

**Approach C — Pragmatic hybrid**, built on a layered (medallion) pipeline.

**Architecture: Bronze → Silver → Gold**
- **Bronze (raw landing):** every fetch lands exactly as received (raw JSON/CSV),
  immutable, partitioned by `source/date`. Enables reprocessing without
  re-scraping.
- **Silver (conformed):** parsed, typed, deduped, entity-resolved into canonical
  IDs. One clean row per real-world thing.
- **Gold (features):** match-level feature tables; recency weights, rolling form,
  and Elo computed here, not in storage.

**Schema (star, around a match spine):**
```
dim_team       (team_id PK, canonical name, ISO code, confederation)
dim_player     (player_id PK, name, birthdate, nationality)
dim_competition(comp_id, name, type: club|international, tier)
dim_season     (season_id, comp_id, start, end)
fact_match     (match_id PK, date, comp_id, home_team_id, away_team_id,
                home_goals, away_goals, venue, neutral_site,
                closing_1x2_home/draw/away)   -- derived convenience only;
                                              -- fact_odds is canonical
fact_match_team_stats (match_id, team_id, possession, shots, shots_on_target,
                       xg, passes, pass_pct, corners, fouls)
fact_lineup    (match_id, team_id, player_id, position, is_starter, minutes)
fact_player_match_stats (match_id, player_id, goals, assists, shots, xg, saves)
                                              -- DEFERRED beyond Step 1
fact_odds      (match_id, bookmaker, market, selection, line, price,
                captured_at)   -- long format, time series, keeps line movement
                               -- market ∈ {1x2, over_under, asian_handicap}
                               -- 1x2: selection ∈ {home,draw,away}, line=NULL
                               -- over_under: selection ∈ {over,under}, line=2.5 etc
bridge_player_season (player_id, season_id, club_team_id, goals, xg, minutes)
source_id_map  (canonical_id, entity_type, source, source_native_id)
```

The long-format `fact_odds` is what makes goal / over-under predictions
storable: the product promises O/U and correct-score outputs, so the odds table
must hold O/U and handicap markets, not just 1X2. `fact_match` keeps only a
derived closing 1X2 line for convenience; `fact_odds` is the single source of
truth (it carries line movement via `captured_at`).

Two load-bearing pieces:
- **`source_id_map`** is the crosswalk: each source's native ID maps to a canonical
  ID. Never trust two sources to agree on names.
- **`bridge_player_season`** attaches each player's CLUB-season form (FBref) to his
  national lineup — the recency signal the user wants, sourced where it actually
  lives.

**Entity resolution:**
- Teams (~210 national teams): hand-built seed mapping keyed on ISO country code.
- Players: match on `normalize(name) + birthdate + nationality`, fuzzy match with
  `rapidfuzz`, route low-confidence pairs to a manual review queue. Never
  auto-merge ambiguous players.

**Recency:** store a real `date` on every fact; compute `exp(-ξ·days_ago)` at
feature-build time (`days_ago` in days, `ξ` tunable) so it stays adjustable
without reloading. Seed with a ~12-month half-life (ξ ≈ 0.0019/day, i.e.
ln(2)/365); tune later against the backtest.

**`bridge_player_season` join rule:** a national match on `fact_match.date` joins
to the club season **containing or immediately preceding** that date — so a
player's form reflects the season he was actually in when the international was
played.

**Manual review queue:** a `manual_review` DuckDB table (`candidate_a`,
`candidate_b`, `score`, `status ∈ {pending,merge,reject}`) holding sub-threshold
player matches for human resolution. Nothing auto-merges below the confidence cut.

**Stack (solo, local-first):**
- DuckDB warehouse (single file, reads Parquet/CSV, fast analytical SQL).
- Python + Polars for ingestion (httpx, retries, rate-limit handling).
- dbt-duckdb for silver→gold transforms with free data-quality tests
  (`unique`, `not_null`, `relationships`, `accepted_values`) plus a reconciliation
  test (sum of player goals == match goals).
- Parquet files in `/bronze`; Makefile/Python entrypoints for orchestration
  (no Airflow until there's pain).

**Build order:**
1. Spine: international_results → `fact_match` + **computed national-team Elo**
   (rolled from the results themselves; clubelo rates *club* teams only and can't
   rate Brazil/Morocco — eng review issue 1). Build the ~210-team ISO seed map +
   reconciliation tripwire here too (eng review issue 3 — entity resolution isn't
   deferrable once two sources join). (Usable dataset.)
2. Odds: The Odds API (live) + football-data.co.uk (historical closing) →
   `fact_odds`. Live WC capture is a **robust cron + dead-man alert + OddsPortal
   fallback** (eng review issue 2 — missed window = data lost 4 years).
   (Now backtestable — see odds caveat below.)
3. Richness: StatsBomb open data first (free Parquet, no scraping) → team stats +
   `bridge_player_season`; FBref best-effort. `fact_player_match_stats`
   (per-player-per-match) is DEFERRED beyond Step 1 — it's the heaviest table and
   the doc rates player stats "secondary signal, largely already priced." Keep
   `bridge_player_season` (the real recency signal); skip per-match player rows.

**Bronze immutability + intra-day odds:** odds update through the day, so the
odds bronze layer is **append-only**, partitioned by `source/date/captured_at` —
each fetch is a new immutable snapshot, not an overwrite. Results/stats bronze
stays one-file-per-`source/date`.

## Data Sources (ranked by what they unlock)

- **football-data.co.uk** — free historical *closing* odds, but **club leagues
  only** — no internationals / World Cup. Good for proving the backtest harness on
  league data, not for WC-specific history.
- **The Odds API** (the-odds-api.com) — live odds free tier (~500 req/mo). Note:
  **historical odds / line movement are a paid add-on**, not free. Budget for this
  if you want real WC backtest fuel at the line-movement granularity.
- **StatsBomb open data** — free Parquet/JSON, NO scraping; covers SELECT
  competitions (some past World Cups, a few leagues), not broad club coverage for
  ~210 nations' players. Use first for Step-3 richness.
- **FBref** — xG, shots, possession, pass %, keeper stats; richest free club data
  BUT aggressively rate-limited and scrapers break often. Treat as best-effort,
  not a load-bearing dependency.
- **martj42/international_results** — international results spine (no player stats,
  no odds).
- **National-team Elo** — computed by us from the international results spine
  (`new = old + K·(result − expected)`), recency-aware and tunable. NOTE: clubelo.com
  is CLUB teams only — not usable for national sides; validate our numbers against
  eloratings.net (World Football Elo) instead.
- **API-Football** (api-sports.io) — lineups, injuries, player + live stats in one
  API (the Approach-A fallback if multi-source scraping stalls).

## Success Criteria (this session's scope — the data layer)

- A DuckDB database with the star schema populated for: international results +
  Elo (phase 1), odds joined to matches (phase 2), team + player advanced stats
  (phase 3).
- dbt tests pass, including the reconciliation test (player goals sum == match
  goals).
- Every `fact` row carries a real `date`; odds are stored as a time series.
- One query can return, for a given upcoming match: both teams' current Elo,
  recent-form features, the starting lineups, each player's club-season stats, and
  the current bookmaker odds across books.

## The Assignment

Before writing any scraper, do this one thing: **pull one week of historical
closing odds from football-data.co.uk for any league, load it into a DuckDB
`fact_odds` table next to the match results, and write the single SQL query that
computes the market's implied probability (1/odds, normalized to remove the vig)
for each match.** It takes an afternoon, it forces the schema decision that
matters most, and it puts the one column on the table — odds — that lets every
future model answer the only question that pays: *am I better than the market?*
Do this before you build the big player-stats pipeline.
